In more than 10 years of hosting our weekly Digital Lifestyle Show, we have seen many methods of storing and playing media (be that music, video, or photos) come and go.

At the start, most of our listeners stored their media, if they did so at all, on a Windows desktop PC and usually consumed it on the same PC. They might have had some sort of MP3 player, and iPods were beginning to make an impact, but it certainly wasn’t the diverse marketplace it is now.

We have seen the growth of both Apple’s iOS and Google’s Android platforms, the birth of downloadable media, and in recent years the surge of streaming services such as Netflix.

While in those early days we had a dream of one integrated system where we could listen to and view our media on our desktop, phone, and even games platform, this has dissolved into a world where media is usually tied to the technology family you most use, be it Microsoft, Apple, or Google.

Even within Microsoft’s platforms it has become increasingly difficult to find one solution to share your media.

This is why we are really excited by the opportunities Windows 10 brings. It has the promise of a universal Windows platform. This is a way of allowing apps to run on any variant of Windows 10 on any platform it supports. Windows 10 supports a lot of platforms from desktops and laptops to tablets and phones to even Xbox One and Raspberry Pi 2.

Microsoft has also cleverly made some of its core media apps from Windows 10 available on other platforms including Apple and Google.

Where your media comes from and how it is stored will still depend whether at heart you are a Microsoft, Apple, or Google devotee, but Windows 10 holds out the hope of being able to consume that media everywhere.

This book will help show you how to fulfill that ambition.

1. Music Services on the Go

If music be the food of love, play on, so said Duke Orsino in Shakespeare’s Twelfth Night. Our love of music is still as strong today as it was back then. However, we often can struggle to play our music where we want. Windows 10, coupled with the ability to use Microsoft’s music apps on Android and Apple devices, looks to solve that quandary. You can now take your music anywhere.

With Windows 10, you can enjoy music on your PC, laptop, tablet, or phone with a range of services.

In this chapter, you will look at how to download, stream, and purchase music on your Android, iOS, or Windows phone, tablet, or laptop with Microsoft Groove and third-party services. You will also find out how you can listen to radio stations and podcasts on the move.

Listening to Your Music with Groove

Information

Microsoft changed the name of its music app in Windows 10 from Xbox Music, as it was known in Windows 8, to Groove. You may find that on some platforms with older versions it is still referred to as Xbox Music.

Groove is Microsoft’s music service that combines a number of features into one service that works with Windows 10, Xbox One, iPad, and Android devices.

With the services you can listen to your own music stored on a PC or phone, stream music from the cloud, and purchase music directly from your device.

Groove breaks down into four areas: listening to music stored on the device, streaming music from your personal collection on Microsoft’s servers, purchasing albums and songs from Microsoft, and using Microsoft’s music subscription service.

Groove on a PC or Laptop

When you think of taking music on the move, you might be tempted to limit your thoughts to just phones or portable media players such as the iPod Touch. That would be wrong. Feedback from listeners to our weekly The Digital Lifestyle show reveals that a surprising amount of music playback on the go still occurs on Windows devices, such as the Surface line, and laptops. These will be running the desktop or PC version of Windows 10, and in this section you will look at what this offers for you to play your music.

Once you open the app, you are presented with a view of your collection of music stored on your PC (Figure 1-1).

Playing Music

The music app can display your collection in different views including by album, artist, song, or playlist. You can pick your preferred view by selecting the relevant icon on the left side of the app. If the menu is not visible, you can use the menu button at the top left of the window to toggle it open.

Information

The menu button looks like three horizontal lines on top of each other. Some people think this looks like a hamburger, and thus this type of menu is often referred to as a hamburger menu (shown earlier in Figure 1-1).

In the Artists view, the artists are sorted alphabetically, and selecting a letter brings up the alphabet where you can then jump to another letter, making it easy to quickly find your artists if you have a large collection. When you select an artist, the app shows the albums and song in your collection from those artists. You can either select Play, which is a rightward-pointing triangle, to play the songs from the artist or select the plus sign to add the songs to a playlist (more on that in a minute).

In the Albums view (Figures 1-4 and 1-5), each album is represented by a thumbnail of the album cover. Clicking the cover shows the album details and track listing. Pressing the Play icon, a rightward-pointing triangle, will play whole album, or you can select an individual track by clicking it and pressing the Play button.

Figure 1-5.

Albums view looks different on a phone or small tablet

Figure 1-4.

Artist’s Albums view, desktop

You can also add a song or the whole album to a playlist by clicking/tapping the plus icon.

Information

A playlist is a collection of songs that you can play back later. For example, you could create a list of songs for working out to or songs you like driving to. They can be any combination of music you like. To create a new playlist, you click/tap the plus icon in the icon list on the right side.

You can then enter a name for the playlist and click on Save. This creates a new playlist and then takes you back to your music collection.

When you click/tap a song, you will see a plus sign, which presents you with a list of your playlists; you can select the playlist you want to add the song to.

If you want to add a selection of songs, albums, or artists to a playlist or download a group of songs, there is an easy way to do it. Switch to the desired section by selecting either the Albums, Artists, or Songs view from the left menu. Then you can click/tap the Select button at the top.

Note

The Select button in Windows 10 is often shown as four horizontal lines on top of each other with two check marks beside them. You can see this at the top right of Figure 1-5 .

Then click/tap the songs/albums you want to select (a check mark shows a selected file). When you select content, a menu bar will appear at the bottom of the screen. You can also select multiple tracks by holding down the Ctrl key and clicking the desired items (on a PC Only).

On the menu bar you will see a Play button, which will play the selected songs. There is a plus icon, which enables you to add the selected songs to a playlist. There is also a Download button, which downloads the selected songs from the cloud to your local device (more on that later). You can also use the select button to delete a group of songs.

At the top of most screens you will notice there is an option for filtering.

With the Filter button, you can choose whether to show your entire music collection or narrow it to the music stored on your local device, streaming, or on OneDrive (see Figure 1-6).

Figure 1-6.

Filtering

The All option does what you expect and shows your entire collection. The “Available offline” option shows music stored only on the PC (available to listen to without being on the Internet), which includes music stored on your device as well as songs you have previously streamed from the cloud and are cached on your device.

The Streaming option shows music stored on OneDrive or available as part of your Groove subscription. The “Only on this device” option filters the list to show just music on the device you are using.

The On OneDrive storage option is something you will learn about in Chapter 2. It’s basically Microsoft’s free cloud storage where you can store your music collection on Microsoft’s servers and access the content from any of your devices or the Web.

You can also choose how the app should sort and arrange your music. There is a Sort By link where you can select from the following options: Date added, A to Z, Release year, Genre (type of music), Artist, or Album.

There is a “Shuffle all” button on the top left. Selecting “Shuffle all” starts playing music within the current view in a random order. This feature is great when you are not sure what kind of music you are in the mood for.

When you play a song, the Now Playing bar (Figure 1-7) at the bottom of the app shows the progress of the current song and has Previous Track, Play/Pause, Next Track, Volume, Shuffle, and Repeat buttons, in that order.

Figure 1-7.

The Now Playing bar at the bottom of the screen Note

Because there is little space on the Now Playing bar, the buttons to control playback use icons rather than words (see Figure 1-7), but they are in the order mentioned.

The Shuffle button plays your current song selection in random order; for instance, if you are playing an album, the shuffle mode will play the songs in a random order. The Repeat button tells the app to play the current song list again after it gets to the end of the current playlist (or album) rather than stop at the end.

Another option is the “Now playing” view, accessible from the left menu. This displays the current song selection as a list along with album artwork. In this view there is also a button (see Figure 1-8) to show the “Now playing” view in full-screen. This shows a slide show of artist artwork that looks especially good on a tablet.

Figure 1-8.

“Now playing” view with Switch to full screen button highlighted Information

The “Now playing” full-screen view is also good when used on large-screen cathode-ray tube (CRT) and plasma screens because it helps prevent burn-in.

When a music track changes or you change the volume, the app displays a notification showing the volume and the current track.

In this section, you learned how to play your music and the useful options displayed while playing that music. In the next section, you’ll look at a great way of accessing particular music quickly.

Pinning Content to the Start Menu

Another great feature of Groove is the ability to pin artists, playlists, and songs to the Windows 10 Start menu. This makes it easy to start playing your favorite tracks directly from the Start menu.

To add an album, artist, or playlist to the Start menu, follow these steps:

Find what you want to add from the Groove app by clicking the album, artist, or playlist name.   Click the More button.   This will give you a pop-up menu where you will see the “Pin to start” option. Selecting this will add the album to the Start menu for easy access.  

You have now seen how to add music to your collection, play that music, and access it quickly. But what if you want to have access to music that is not currently in your collection? In the next few sections, you will learn how Groove in Windows 10 makes it easy to obtain music.

Purchasing Songs and Music Subscriptions

As well as Groove playing music stored on your tablet, PC, or the cloud, it also has a library of songs you can purchase. You can purchase a single song or an album. If you want unlimited access to music, there is something called a Groove Music Pass subscription.

Groove Music Pass

The Music Pass subscription is a service that you pay for monthly. With a subscription you can listen to or download any of the songs and albums available in Groove collections. You can browse the Groove Music collection in the Windows Store (as shown earlier in Figure 1-3) for songs, artists, and albums and play them on your phone or tablet.

When you subscribe, you typically pay around $10 (£7) per month and can access the subscription from any of your devices. So, you can subscribe once and then listen to the music on your Android, iPhone, or Windows phone, tablet, and PC. If you buy a lot of music, then this is a service worth looking at. There is a 30-day free trial available also; you can find out more at https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/groove-music .

Tip

In some countries, Microsoft’s store will often have bundle offers including Office 365 and a Groove Music Pass subscription at a discount. These can be worth looking out for.

Managing Your Devices

If you have a Groove Music Pass subscription, you can stream music on any of your Windows 10 devices as well as iOS, Android, and Xbox.

As you saw earlier in the chapter, you can also download music to play when you’re offline. However, there is a four-device limit with Groove, so you can download tracks only from four devices (including iOS, Android, and Windows devices).

The first time you download a song from a device, the device registers it with Groove, and each time you download a song on a new device, the device registers with Groove. Once you have four devices registered, you can’t download from another device; you can still stream music but not download it.

If you get rid of a phone or PC and want a new device to be registered in its place, you can remove a device from the list. You can do this by going to the Groove Devices page, which you can launch from Settings in the Groove app.

The Devices page (Figure 1-9) lists the PC, phones, and tablets registered with Groove and the date they were registered.

Figure 1-9.

Managing your devices for Groove

You can click/tap the Remove button, and it will ask you to confirm your selection. When you do that, it will deregister the device, and you will still be able to stream music on it but not download music.

Note

You can remove only one device every 30 days.

You have now seen how to sign up for a Groove subscription and how to manage what devices you can use that subscription on. In the next section, you will look at how that subscription can improve your Groove experience.

Exploring Music

Note

The Exploring Music option is currently unavailable in Windows 10 Mobile.

Information Windows 10 Mobile is the edition of Windows 10 used on phones and small tablets. Microsoft defines a small tablet as one having a screen size of less than 8 inches diagonally.

If you have a Groove Music Pass, there is another section in the Groove app, labeled Explorer. In this section you can explore (hence the name) the latest music from the Groove Music store. When you open this section, it presents a selection of new music, the current top songs, the top artist, and the top albums.

You can select an album and start playing it exactly as you could if it was one of your own albums. You can add it to your playlists, start a radio station, and download tracks.

Information

Radio stations in Groove and many of the other streaming services are not traditional broadcasters. Instead, they are a way of producing a random playlist drawn from all available music on the streaming service. These playlists are usually themed around an artist or genre. Examples might be artists like Dire Straits or music from the 1970s.

If you select an artist, you can view albums by artist, and just as you can if you viewed an artist in your collection, you can play and download albums and songs by artist.

Buying Music

You’ve already learned about Groove subscriptions, but if you do not want to pay a monthly amount and do not have a Groove Music Pass, you can still browse the store and pay for the songs and albums you want to buy.

From the Windows Store app (Figure 1-10), you can click/tap the Music link and then browse Microsoft’s collection of music.

Figure 1-10.

The Windows Store

When you see a single song or an album that you are interested in, click/tap the Buy icon, and you will be able to purchase the content. The cost will be charged to your Microsoft account in the same way buying an app would. If you have a Music Pass subscription, you will see a Download button instead of a Buy button. Selecting Download will download the selection to your PC.

Once you have bought a song or album, it is automatically added to your collection and available to play on your other devices by streaming it over the Internet. You can also download your purchases to your other devices so you can listen to them without being connected to the internet.

Note

If you have iOS devices, Android devices, or a Windows phone, you can still access your music collection from them. Later in this chapter you will see how to access your collection on Android devices, iPhones, and Windows 10 phones.

Groove Radio

Note

You must have an active Groove Music Pass subscription for the radio option to be available.

Groove has a music discovery service that will create a custom radio station based on an artist. To start a station, follow these steps:

Select an artist in the music app via the Artists view.   Click/tap the Start Radio button that appears.  

Groove will create a playlist based on the artist you have selected. You will get some songs from the select artist along with tracks from other artists Groove thinks are similar; you can view the playlist by going to the “Now playing” view in the Groove app. As you listen to songs, the app adds more songs to the playlist.

When you create a radio station, it gets added to the Radio section of the app. If you go to the Radio section, you can view the radio stations you have created. If you select “Start a station,” you can type in the name of an artist or select from popular artists to create a new station.

Searching for Music

With Groove, you can search for songs, artist, or albums via the search box. Just type your search criteria into the box, and Groove will start showing search suggestions. Selecting one of the results will bring up the search results page. Here it will list matching artists, albums, and songs.

If you have a Groove Music Pass, you will see two tabs (Figure 1-11): In collection and Full catalog. The “In collection” tab shows matching content in your own music collection either on the device or in the cloud on OneDrive.

Figure 1-11.

Search results

The “Full catalogue” tab shows content available in the Groove store. If you have a Groove Music Pass subscription, you can download or listen to any of the music shown in the results.

If you don’t have a Groove Music Pass, the app will search your own music collection, and if nothing matches your search criteria, you will see a link that when selected opens the store app and searches the Windows Store.

Settings

On a PC Groove has a number of settings that you can use to customize your experience. The Settings section of the app is split between Music Pass settings and Your Music settings.

Music Pass

This is where you can manage the devices you are using with Groove. Selecting this option takes you to the web page you saw earlier in Figure 1-9 for managing devices.

Your Music

This is where you can manage music files on the PC. The first option is “Choose where we look for music.” Selecting this option brings up the dialog box you saw earlier in the chapter in Figure 1-2 where you can tell the app where you store music on your device.

The following are the other options:

  • Automatically download songs you add from Groove: With this option selected, the PC will automatically download songs you buy (or add via your Groove Music Pass subscription).
  • Automatically retrieve and update missing album art and metadata: With this option, the app will automatically download album artwork, artist information, and other metadata when you add music to your collection.
  • When I add a song to OneDrive, remove any version that came from Groove Music Pass: This option will remove duplicate tracks from your Groove Music Pass subscription.
  • Ask me to sign in before completing purchases or managing my account: This option means that the app will prompt with your password when you purchase new music.

There is also the option “Delete your playlists, and any music you’ve added or download from the music catalogue.” Selecting this will make the app remove content you have purchased or added from Groove and the Music Store.

The final option is a Dark or Light setting. The Light setting gives you black text on a white background, and the Dark setting gives you white text on a black background.

Using Groove on a Windows 10 Mobile Device

Information

Prior to Windows 10, the Windows operating system on phones was known as Windows Phone. Microsoft has changed this to Windows 10 Mobile. A Windows 10 Mobile device can be a phone or a small (less than 8-inch) tablet. For convenience, this book will often refer to these devices as phones.

With Windows 10 on a phone or small tablet, Groove works very much in the same way as the PC version does with just a few changes. In this section, you will learn how to listen to your own music on a Windows 10 mobile device and how to listen using a Groove Music Pass subscription.

You will find the Groove app on your phone’s start screen; if you don’t, you can find it in the “All apps” list.

Playing Music

When you open the music app, it displays your music collection by album (Figure 1-12). You can change the view so that the app displays your music by artist, song, or playlist.

Figure 1-12.

Windows Mobile Albums view

To change to a different view, tap the menu icon on the top left of the app. When you tap the menu icon, it slides out a list where you will see these buttons: Recent Plays, Albums, Artist, Songs, Playlist, Radio, and Explorer. You will look at Radio and Explorer later in this section; for now, tap the Albums view, and it will list your collection alphabetically.

To play an album, tap the Play button located on the album cover image. The app will start playing the first song from the album.

If you tap the album, it will take you to the album page. To play the whole album, tap the Play All button. To play a single track, tap the song name.

The other options in this view are Add To and More. The Add To button adds the album to a playlist or the Now Playing bar, which is the current song selection list. Adding to this will add the songs to the end of Now Playing bar.

To add to a playlist, tap the playlist you want to add to (for information on playlists, see the “Playing Music” section earlier in this chapter).

The More button displays these additional menu options: Delete, See Artist, and Download.

  • The Delete option deletes this album from your collection.
  • See Artist takes you to the artist page (the same as selecting an artist from the Artists view).
  • The Download option shows only if the album you have selected is stored on the cloud. Selecting this option downloads the album to your phone for offline playback. You will look at offline playback and cloud-stored files later in this chapter.

When you start playing an album or song, it will take you to the “Now playing” view. In this view, there are buttons to go back to a previous track, pause playback, and skip to the next track. There is a Repeat button, which when selected will play the current song or playlist again after it has finished playing. The Shuffle button makes the app play back your content in a random order. The final button on this list is a More button, which gives you the option of adding the current song to a playlist.

On the “Now playing” view you will also see a small arrow pointing down. Tapping this button brings up the current playlist.

Filtering and Sorting

When you select Artists, Albums, or Songs from the view menu button, you will see a Refine option at the top of the screen. This option enables you to filter and sort your collection to make it easier to find your music.

The Refine option is split into two sections: Filter and Sort.

The Filter section is split between All, Available Offline, and Streaming. When you have All selected, it will show all your music collection whether on the phone, stored in OneDrive, or as part of your Groove Music Pass subscription.

Note

OneDrive is Microsoft’s cloud storage solution and something you will look at in Chapter 2.

Read more about Groove Music Pass subscriptions earlier in this chapter.

To show music stored only on your phone and available to play without an Internet connection, you select the “Available offline” option. If you want to see music only from OneDrive or your Groove Music Pass subscription, select Streaming.

In the Sort section, you can have the music sorted alphabetically, and if you are in the Albums view, you also get an Artist option.

Note

In any of the Albums, Artists, and Songs views, there is “Shuffle all” button that will play your music in a random order.

Up to now you have been looking at Groove on Windows 10 devices be they PC, tablet, laptop, or phone. What if you have an Android phone or an iPhone? Fortunately, Microsoft has recently become rather surprisingly good at putting its applications on these competing platforms. Groove with Android Devices and iPhones

One of the great things about Microsoft’s Groove music service is that as well as working on Windows PCs, phones, and tablets, it works on Android devices and iPhones. The iOS and Android versions of the app are not designed to be music players for music stored on the phone or tablet but players for music stored in OneDrive or from a Groove Music Pass subscription. See Chapter 2 to learn about storing your music collection in OneDrive.

Android Devices

To use Groove on Android, head to the Google Play Store and search for Groove Music. Once installed, run the app, and it will ask you for your Microsoft account login. This is the account you have used with Windows to upload your collection to OneDrive or you used when you subscribed to a Groove.

Once you have logged into the app, you are presented with your music collection. Your collection can be viewed by artist, album, song, and genre. To view your collection by artist, click the Artists tab (Figure 1-13).

2. Storing Your Music in the Cloud

Information

Over the years playing your music on the move has been one of the biggest challenges facing enthusiasts. When portable music first started being available in electronic forms, you stored your music on your PC or Mac and then physically connected the device, perhaps an iPod or phone, that you wanted to transfer that music to.

This was always a slightly cumbersome and time-consuming process. It also relied on you knowing you wanted to play certain music on the go. People have always wanted to be able to access all their music at any time without having to plan for the event.

One of the really great features of Windows 10 on a PC is that any music you have stored on the device can be synced to the cloud for accessing from other Windows PCs and phones. In fact, as you saw in Chapter 1, Microsoft also has Groove Music apps for iOS and Android. So, you can have your music collection on your PC and instantly have it accessible from Windows, Android, and iOS phones and tablets.

You have a few different options for storing your music in the cloud. Let’s start by looking at Microsoft’s free solution.

Information

Storing music in the cloud can sound mystical, but all it really means is storing your data on a service that is accessible anywhere via the Internet. That service, be it owned by Microsoft, Google, or even Amazon, may have servers located all over the world, and your data may be stored on any one of them (although for privacy and efficiency reasons the server is likely to be physically close to your home location).

The point is you do not need to know where it is stored, only that it is accessible to you anywhere you are via the Internet.

Using OneDrive to Store Your Music

OneDrive is Microsoft’s cloud storage solution that you can use to store any files, including documents, photos, and media files. 3. Watching Videos, Movies, and TV Shows

So far in this book, we have mainly concentrated on how to play music, but we know from our experience gathered from hosting our weekly podcast that many audience questions revolve around the world of playing video and its many confusing sources, formats, and containers. People often just do not get why a file they have downloaded from the Internet does not play.

Information

Sources are video streaming services such as Netflix, video hosting services such as YouTube, and media stored locally or on a network storage device.

Formats are the way the video is encoded for storage. These are often known as codecs (short for coder-decoder). Examples of codecs are MPEG, WMV, and MP4, but there are lots more.

Containers are the cause of a lot of confusion. These are basically a way of packaging up one or more media files for playback.

You could consider, for example, a DVD a container, and indeed a DVD can be converted to an ISO file for playback on a PC, but a DVD can contain many different media types including audio and video.

This is the same with other media containers including the popular MKV container. One issue is that software, including Windows 10, may claim to play back MKV files, but they will play back these containers only if they contain media in formats they understand.

Windows 10 has gone a long way to help this confusion, and we have found it much better at playing some of the more obscure file types. We have found most files we have downloaded just work. One exception is the playback of DVDs, which has been removed. However, Microsoft has now released a Windows DVD player app (available at https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/store/apps/windows-dvd-player/9nblggh2j19w for a fee). Third-party applications such as VLC, which we cover later in this chapter, can also help with this.

Windows 10 has also really helped control the potential cost of watching video streams, particularly when on metered connections such as a mobile phone network.

In this chapter, you will look at all the different ways you can use Windows 10 to watch videos from your own collection, from the Windows Store, from Netflix, and from YouTube.

You will also look at how other video apps including VLC can help you watch all your different video formats.

Finally, you will look how you can use Windows 10’s Data Sense app to monitor and control how much data you are using while streaming video.

5. Streaming Media Around the Home

With Windows 10 you can stream music and videos from media servers on your network using a standard called Digital Living Network Alliance (DLNA). DLNA is a certified standard for media servers, media players, and applications to work together. With DLNA you have a media server and something called a media renderer. The server could be a PC running Windows Media Player, a network-attached storage (NAS) box in your home, or media server software installed on a PC.

In the previous chapter, you learned how you can set up Windows Media Player to work as a media server, and in this chapter you will look at how you can stream from Windows Media Player and other media servers on your home network.

You will also learn how you can send media on your PC to DLNA-compatible devices on your home network using Windows 10, including how some third-party software can help you stream from your Windows 10 phone.

Streaming from a DLNA Server to a Windows PC

When you have media servers set up on your network, you can stream media stored on them from a Windows 10 PC. In the previous chapter, you set up Windows Media Player to work as a DLNA server, and you can also use Windows Media Player to access content on other PCs on your network using DLNA.

Information

DLNA is a standard allowing media devices from different (or the same) manufacturers to share content over the network they are attached to. This standard is controlled by the Digital Living Network Alliance. Quite a few devices support DLNA, including many smart TVs and modern audio systems.

When you run Windows Media Player on a PC running on the same network as other PCs, you will see listed libraries that have been shared from the other PCs (Figure 5-1).

Figure 5-1.

As well as seeing PCs with Windows Media Player (also known as WMP) running in shared mode, you will see other DLNA servers on your network, which could include NAS devices, Sonos devices, and other media servers.

Information

DLNA is based on another standard: Universal Plug and Play Audio Visual (UPnP AV). Some manufacturers produce UPnP devices that will fully comply with the DLNA standard but do not want to call them DLNA because there is a licensing cost involved. This means that while not guaranteed, many UPnP Av devices will work perfectly happily with Windows 10’s DLNA features.

6. Setting Up Media Servers to Stream Your Content

So far in this book you have looked at media streaming around your home network and from the cloud, but what if you have your own music and video collection and want to be able to access it from anywhere around the world? In this chapter, you will look at two solutions that enable streaming your own music and video collection around your home and over the Internet. You will learn how to set up a media server, and then you can access the content on your Windows 10 devices over your home network or the Internet.

The systems you will learn how to set up are the two most popular among listeners to our weekly podcast; they are called Plex and Emby. There are a number of other alternatives. Plex is a media server that is designed to be a simple solution for storing and organizing all your personal media collection and for serving it to Plex clients on your various devices. You install Plex Media Server on a PC, where your music and videos collection is located, and then you can access your collection from various devices including Windows PCs, Windows phones, and iOS and Android devices. Plex is a powerful and flexible media solution, and in this section you will set up a basic server and connect to it via Windows 10.

The app will then list albums by the selected artists; tap the Play button on an album cover, and it will start playing.

In the Artists view, swipe right to view individual songs by the artist; swipe right again to read the artist bio.

As with movies and TV shows, you can send music to another Emby client or DLNA device on your home network using the Play To button at the bottom of the screen. Tap the button, and it will bring up a list of DLNA devices and Emby clients on your home network. Tap the device you want to play; the content will start playing on the remote device.

Tip

There are other features we haven’t covered here that are well worth looking at. Go to http://emby.media for more details.

Using Emby with a Web Browser

As well as the Emby apps for Windows 10, you can use Emby with a web browser for free and without having to install anything on your PC.

From a web browser, you enter the IP address of the PC running Emby Server followed by the port that Emby Server is running on. Rather than trying to work out what your IP address and the port your server is running on, you can find it via your Emby account.

From a web browser, go to http://Emby.media .   Click Sign In and enter your Emby username and password.   Click the Sign In button.   Click your Emby PC.  

You will see a web-based version of the Emby client with which you can browse your media collection and play it via the browser.

Tip

This is a simple way of accessing your media without having to set up anything on your PC.

Emby has many features we haven’t talked about here and is well worth investigating. It can stream live TV, it can integrate with Windows Media Center (on Windows 7 and 8) and other media platforms such as Kodi, and it has a very vibrant community around it. Go to http://emby.media for more information and join the community to get more involved.

Resources for Configuring Media Servers on Other Platforms

In this chapter, you looked at Plex Media Server and Emby Server, which are powerful media servers that also have client apps for many different platforms including Windows 10. In this section, you will take a brief look at two other media servers that do not have specific Windows 10 clients but will run on Windows 10 and a lot of other platforms. They are definitely worth a look. These are Kodi and MediaPortal.

Kodi

Kodi is an open source media server project that was previously known as Xbox Media Center. It started life as a media solution for the original Xbox and has become a popular system. Kodi can be installed on a wide range of platforms including Windows 10 and has media management features, network streaming, and community add-ins. To find out more, go to http://Kodi.tv where you will be able to read about its features, and you will find the download links.

Media Portal

MediaPortal is a media solution designed for a PC. Unlike Plex and Emby, which are client-server systems, MediaPortal is an all-in-one media system that you can connect to a big-screen TV or projector. It supports live TV, recorded TV, music, and movie playback, and it has a wide range of plug-ins available. Go to www.team-mediaportal.com for more details and the download links.
